Horror criticism often talks first about imagery: creatures, gore, uncanny bodies, corrupted houses, impossible faces. In comics, that emphasis can be misleading. The medium's real advantage is not only what it can show, but how precisely it can delay showing it.

## Page turn is a timing machine

Comics give horror an unusually mechanical control over suspense. The page turn can conceal an image, release it, or reveal that the expected image is absent. That last option is often the most effective.

Readers participate in the timing. Their hand completes the beat. Few media can externalize dread so simply.

## Silence is structural, not ornamental

Empty panels, repeated compositions, and slowed action all produce a form of pressure. They are not atmospheric garnish. They tell the reader that something is wrong with the rhythm of the scene.

This is why many horror comics feel stronger in rereading than in summary. The plot may be simple, but the cadence is doing sophisticated work.

## Monsters are only one layer

Great creature design matters, but it is often secondary to staging. A terrifying drawing placed too early, too clearly, or too often can flatten the scene instead of intensifying it. Horror needs visual economy.

The strongest artists know when the monster should remain a rumor, a texture, or a partial intrusion into the frame.

## Why some books linger

Books like *Gideon Falls*, *Harrow County*, or *The Nice House on the Lake* stay with readers because they build unease through rhythm as much as through concept. Their most memorable moments are often not the loudest.

In horror comics, monsters matter. But rhythm decides whether they haunt the page or just occupy it.
